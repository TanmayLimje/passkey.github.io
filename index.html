QUESTION 1

  
What is a passkey?

  

    A passkey is a digital credential that lets you sign in to apps and
    websites without a password — using the same fingerprint, face scan,
    or PIN you use to unlock your device.
  

  
    
    
  • Built on public-key cryptography — a unique key pair replaces the typed secret.
    • 
    
  • One passkey per account, per site — created automatically; never invented or typed by you.
    • 
    
  • Unlocked locally — your biometrics stay on your device and are never sent to the website.
    • 
    
  • An open standard — based on FIDO2 / WebAuthn, not owned by any single company.
    • 
  







  !
  
QUESTION 2 — THE NEED

  
Why do we need passkeys?

  
Passwords were invented in 1961 — and the internet has outgrown them.

  

    

      
Humans are predictable

      
We choose weak passwords and reuse them everywhere. Attackers exploit this with
         credential stuffing: try leaked passwords on many other sites.

    

    

      
Phishing is rampant

      
Fake login pages steal credentials every day. Even careful users get fooled —
         and SMS one-time codes can be phished or intercepted too.

    

    

      
Servers get breached

      
Websites must store your secret (or its hash). Every major breach dumps millions
         of credentials onto the dark web.

    

  

  

    The root problem: a password is a shared secret — both you AND the
    server know it. Whoever steals it, from either side, becomes you. The industry needed a login
    with no shared secret, nothing to phish, and nothing to reuse. That is exactly what a passkey is.
  







  
QUESTION 2 — THE HISTORY

  
How did passkeys emerge?

  

    

      
1961

      

        
Passwords are born

        
MIT's CTSS computer introduces passwords for shared machines — the "shared secret" era begins.

      

    

    

      
2012

      

        
FIDO Alliance founded

        
Tech companies (PayPal, Lenovo and others) unite with one goal: kill the password using cryptography.

      

    

    

      
2019

      

        
WebAuthn becomes a standard

        
The W3C approves WebAuthn; together with FIDO's CTAP it forms FIDO2 — the plumbing behind passkeys.

      

    

    

      
2022

      

        
The big three go all-in

        
Apple, Google & Microsoft jointly announce passkey support; it ships in iOS 16, Android and Windows.

      

    

    

      
2023 →

      

        
Mass adoption

        
Google makes passkeys the default; WhatsApp, PayPal, Amazon, GitHub and hundreds more follow.

QUESTION 3

  
Does it replace passwords & traditional authentication?

  

    Yes — that is the goal. A passkey is designed to replace the password
    entirely, not sit next to it. During the transition, most sites keep passwords as a fallback.
  

  

    
    
      
  • Replaces passwords — sign in with a touch or glance instead of typing.
    • 
      
  • Replaces SMS / app OTP codes — a passkey already proves two things:
          your device (have) + biometrics/PIN (are/know). It is built-in 2FA.
    • 
      
  • Replaces security questions — no "first pet's name" to guess or leak.
    • 
    

    
    
      
  • …but not overnight — many sites and older systems still require passwords today.
    • 
      
  • Fallbacks remain — keep your account recovery options up to date during the transition.
    • 
      
  • Hardware keys remain — device-bound passkeys on security keys serve high-security needs.
    • 
    

  







  
QUESTION 4 — PART 1

  
How is a passkey generated?

  

    

      
1

      

        
You opt in

        
On a supported site, choose "Create a passkey" in the account's security settings.

      

    

    

      
2

      

        
Your device makes a key pair

        
The device's secure chip generates a unique public + private key pair for that
           exact website. You never see or choose anything.

      

    

    

      
3

      

        
The public key goes to the site

        
The website stores only your public key — like giving the site a padlock only you can open.

      

    

    

      
4

      

        
The private key stays with you

        
It never leaves your device's secure storage (and syncs only in encrypted form).
           At login, it signs a one-time challenge to prove it's you.

QUESTION 5

  
Where is a passkey stored — device-specific or user-specific?

  

    

      
Synced passkeys → user-specific

      
Stored in your personal cloud account — iCloud Keychain (Apple), Google Password
         Manager, or Microsoft — and synced end-to-end encrypted across your
         phone, tablet and laptop.

      
The passkey belongs to you, not to one device.

    

    

      
Device-bound passkeys → device-specific

      
Stored on a single piece of hardware — e.g., a YubiKey security key or a work
         laptop — and never copied anywhere.

      
Maximum security, but if that device is gone, so is the passkey.

    

  

  

    So which is it? Both exist. For everyday accounts, passkeys are
    user-specific credentials that live on all your devices via encrypted sync (third-party
    managers like 1Password and Bitwarden work too). For high-security needs, they can be
    locked to one device on purpose. Either way: the private key is never visible to you,
    apps, or websites.
  







  
QUESTION 6

  
What if you forget your passkey?

  

    Good news: you can't forget a passkey — there is nothing to remember.
    No string to memorize, nothing on a sticky note. Your device holds it; your fingerprint,
    face, or PIN unlocks it.
  

  

    
    
      
  • Forgot your device PIN? — use biometrics, or recover the device through your Apple / Google / Microsoft account.
    • 
      
  • Biometrics not working? — the device PIN is always there as the local fallback.
    • 
    

    
    
      
  • Can't access the passkey at all? — use the website's account recovery: recovery email/phone, backup codes, or another signed-in device.
    • 
      
  • Then re-enroll — create a fresh passkey in seconds and remove the old one.
    •

QUESTION 7

  
Dos and Don'ts of passkeys

  

    

      
DO

      
    
        
  • Start with key accounts — email, banking, work logins first.
    • 
        
  • Keep devices updated — OS and browser updates carry passkey improvements.
    • 
        
  • Lock your device — a passkey is only as safe as your device PIN/biometrics.
    • 
        
  • Set up recovery — recovery email, phone, and backup codes — before you need them.
    • 
        
  • Register a second device — or a hardware key as backup where supported.
    • 
      

    

    

      
DON'T

      
    
        
  • Share your device PIN — whoever can unlock your device can use your passkeys.
    • 
        
  • Rely on one device only — no backup = painful recovery if it's lost.
    • 
        
  • Create passkeys on shared devices — use your own phone/laptop instead.
    • 
        
  • Delete the password too early — wait until passkey sign-in works on all your devices.
    • 
        
  • Ignore recovery options — an account without recovery is a future lockout.
    • 
      

    

  







  
QUESTION 8

  
What if you lose or destroy your device?

  
It depends on which kind of passkey you had — this is where Question 5 pays off.

  

    

      
Synced passkeys — no drama

      
    
        
  1. Get a new device.
    2. 
        
  2. Sign in to your Apple / Google / Microsoft account.
    3. 
        
  3. Verify your identity (SMS, existing device, account recovery).
    4. 
        
  4. Your passkeys sync back automatically.
    5. 
      

      
Then open the lost device's account page and revoke its access.

    

    

      
Device-bound passkeys — plan B

      
That passkey is gone with the device. You fall back on what you prepared:

      
    
        
  • A second registered device or backup security key.
    • 
        
  • The website's account recovery (email, phone, codes).
    • 
        
  • Then create a new passkey and remove the lost one.
    • 
      

    

  

  

    Moral of the story: losing a device is an inconvenience, not a catastrophe —
    if you set up sync + recovery options beforehand. Two minutes of setup today
    saves a lockout tomorrow.
  







  
SUMMARY

  
Key takeaways

  
    
    
  1. A passkey replaces the shared-secret password with a cryptographic key pair on your devices.
    2. 
    
  2. It emerged from the FIDO Alliance (2012) and WebAuthn standard (2019); Apple, Google & Microsoft brought it mainstream in 2022.
    3. 
    
  3. It's stronger by design: phishing-resistant, unique per site, and nothing valuable leaks in a breach.
    4. 
    
  4. Synced passkeys are user-specific (roam across your devices); device-bound ones live on a single key — by choice.
    5. 
    
  5. You can't forget a passkey; losing a device is recoverable if you set up sync, a second device, and recovery options.
    6. 
  









  
Try it yourself: create a passkey on your Google or Apple account today — it takes under a minute.

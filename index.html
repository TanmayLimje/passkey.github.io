/ question 01

What is a passkey?

A passkey is a digital credential that lets you sign in to apps and websites without a password — using the same fingerprint, face scan, or PIN you use to unlock your device.

Public-key cryptography

A unique key pair replaces the typed secret — math instead of memory.

One per account, per site

Created automatically for each website. Never invented or typed by you.

Unlocked locally

Your biometrics stay on your device and are never sent to the website.

An open standard

Based on FIDO2 / WebAuthn — not owned by any single company.

02

/ question 02

Why do we need it?

Passwords were invented in 1961 — and the internet has outgrown them.

Humans are predictable

We pick weak passwords and reuse them. Attackers exploit this with credential stuffing: try leaked passwords on many other sites.

Phishing is rampant

Fake login pages steal credentials every day. Even SMS one-time codes can be phished or intercepted.

Servers get breached

Websites must store your secret (or its hash). Every breach dumps millions of credentials onto the dark web.

The root problem: a password is a shared secret — both you AND the server know it. Whoever steals it, from either side, becomes you. The industry needed a login with no shared secret, nothing to phish, nothing to reuse. That is exactly what a passkey is.
02

/ question 02 — history

How did it emerge?

1961

Passwords are born

MIT's CTSS computer introduces passwords for shared machines — the "shared secret" era begins.

2012

FIDO Alliance founded

Tech companies (PayPal, Lenovo and others) unite with one goal: kill the password using cryptography.

2019

WebAuthn becomes a standard

The W3C approves WebAuthn; with FIDO's CTAP it forms FIDO2 — the plumbing behind passkeys.

2022

The big three go all-in

Apple, Google & Microsoft jointly announce passkey support; it ships in iOS 16, Android and Windows.

2023+

Mass adoption

Google makes passkeys the default; WhatsApp, PayPal, Amazon, GitHub and hundreds more follow.

03

/ question 03

Does it replace passwords?

Yes — that is the goal. A passkey is designed to replace the password entirely, not sit next to it. During the transition, most sites keep passwords as a fallback.

What it replaces

  • Passwords — sign in with a touch or glance instead of typing.
  • SMS / app OTP codes — a passkey already proves two things: your device (have) + biometrics/PIN (are/know). Built-in 2FA.
  • Security questions — no "first pet's name" to guess or leak.

Reality check

  • Not overnight — many sites and older systems still require passwords today.
  • Fallbacks remain — keep recovery options up to date during the transition.
  • Hardware keys remain — device-bound passkeys serve high-security needs.

/ question 04 — part 1

How is it generated?

1

You opt in

Choose "Create a passkey" in a site's security settings.

2

Your device makes a key pair

Its secure chip generates a unique public + private key pair for that exact website. You never see or choose anything.

3

The public key goes to the site

Like giving the site a padlock only you can open.

4

The private key stays with you

Never leaves secure storage (and syncs only encrypted). At login it signs a one-time challenge to prove it's you.

/ question 05

Where is it stored?

Synced → user-specific

Stored in your personal cloud account — iCloud Keychain, Google Password Manager, or Microsoft — and synced end-to-end encrypted across your phone, tablet and laptop.

The passkey belongs to you, not to one device.

Device-bound → device-specific

Stored on a single piece of hardware — e.g., a YubiKey security key or a work laptop — and never copied anywhere.

Maximum security, but if that device is gone, so is the passkey.

So which is it? Both exist. Everyday passkeys are user-specific and roam across your devices via encrypted sync (1Password, Bitwarden work too). High-security ones can be locked to one device on purpose. Either way, the private key is never visible to you, apps, or websites.
06

/ question 06

What if you forget it?

You can't forget a passkey — there is nothing to remember. No string to memorize, nothing on a sticky note. Your device holds it; your fingerprint, face, or PIN unlocks it.

Forgot your device PIN?

Use biometrics, or recover the device through your Apple / Google / Microsoft account. If biometrics fail, the device PIN is the local fallback.

No access at all?

Use the website's account recovery — recovery email/phone, backup codes, or another signed-in device — then create a fresh passkey in seconds.

07

/ question 07

Dos & Don'ts

DO

  • Start with key accounts — email, banking, work logins first.
  • Keep devices updated — updates carry passkey improvements.
  • Lock your device — a passkey is only as safe as your device PIN/biometrics.
  • Set up recovery — email, phone, backup codes — before you need them.
  • Register a second device — or a hardware key as backup where supported.

DON'T

  • Share your device PIN — whoever unlocks your device can use your passkeys.
  • Rely on one device only — no backup = painful recovery if it's lost.
  • Create passkeys on shared devices — use your own phone/laptop instead.
  • Delete the password too early — wait until passkeys work on all your devices.
  • Ignore recovery options — an account without recovery is a future lockout.
08

/ question 08

Lost or destroyed device?

It depends on which kind of passkey you had — this is where Question 5 pays off.

Synced passkeys — no drama

  1. Get a new device.
  2. Sign in to your Apple / Google / Microsoft account.
  3. Verify your identity (SMS, existing device, recovery).
  4. Your passkeys sync back automatically.

Then open the lost device's account page and revoke its access.

Device-bound — plan B

That passkey is gone with the device. You fall back on what you prepared:

  • A second registered device or backup security key.
  • The website's account recovery (email, phone, codes).
  • Then create a new passkey and remove the lost one.
Moral of the story: losing a device is an inconvenience, not a catastrophe — if you set up sync + recovery options beforehand. Two minutes of setup today saves a lockout tomorrow.

/ summary

Key takeaways

  1. A passkey replaces the shared-secret password with a cryptographic key pair on your devices.
  2. It emerged from the FIDO Alliance (2012) and WebAuthn standard (2019); Apple, Google & Microsoft brought it mainstream in 2022.
  3. It's stronger by design: phishing-resistant, unique per site, and nothing valuable leaks in a breach.
  4. Synced passkeys are user-specific; device-bound ones live on a single key — by choice.
  5. You can't forget a passkey; losing a device is recoverable if you set up sync, a second device, and recovery options.
